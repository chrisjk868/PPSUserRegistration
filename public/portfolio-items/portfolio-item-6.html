Week 6Vickrey Auctions

By Bruce Changlong Xu

March 27, 2021

A Vickrey auction is a spin on the traditional notion of an auction, where a group of individuals bid successively higher prices to purchase a good that has been placed on sale. What happens is that all of the bidders submit their bids on a slip of paper without knowledge of anyone else’s bid price – the highest bidder obtains the good but only needs to pay the price of the second highest bid. This type of auctioning system is supposedly socially optimal, by giving bidders an incentive to put forth exactly their own valuations for said good; and if not, harming themselves by the exact amount of deviation from this value. For example, say an individual is willing to put forth at most 50USD to purchase a particular textbook for the course that they are taking next semester. If they bid a value of 55USD, they run the risk of overpaying past their threshold of tolerance by < 5USD, as the second highest bidder could potentially bid between 51 – 54 USD. In a similar vein, suppose the individual bids only 45USD and loses the bid to someone who put forth 49USD. The highest bidder now only needs to pay 45USD and obtains the textbook, which was a situation that our individual would have been happy to be in. In either case, they run the risk of losing out on the optimal scenario if they do not place forward their true valuation.

Whilst in theory this form of auction is quite appealing, it is quite difficult to implement practically. Bidders are often unwilling to disclose their information to a third party (auctioneer) and may not trust what they say about the second-highest bid. As a result, other auction implementations are more common in the real world. The First Price Sealed Bid Auction simply mandates that the highest bidder pays whatever price that they put forward. This has been shown to be game-theoretically equivalent to the Dutch Auction, which initiates a price that the auctioneer sets, that is continually lowered until a bidder accepts the current price. Both auctions hinge upon the tradeoff between the price that a bidder needs to pay and the probability of holding the highest bid, implying equivalent Bayesian Nash equilibrium bid functions.

Paul Milgrom studied Vickrey Auctions and formulated the linkage principle throughout his tenure at Northwestern, forming the canon of much of modern-day auction theory. Much of this theoretical work influences the way you and I live and work on a daily basis – the way amazon prices its goods, the way Google Search operates, whether AT&T or Verizon gets priority over broadcast signals and more. Auctions are everywhere and having an awareness of how they work will do wonders.
